Abstract

The working thesis, ahead of the full abstract.

Preliminary · full abstract coming with the paper

Helicyn's working thesis is that AI data center infrastructure is increasingly constrained by coordination across compute, energy, cooling, and grid conditions rather than by any single layer in isolation, and that a coordination layer can reduce waste without sacrificing utilization, evaluated so far only in an independent, discrete-time simulator under documented modeling assumptions, not in production.

Preliminary methodology summary

The current evidence base is an independent, discrete-time data-center scheduling simulator (helicyn-sim) that models workload placement, thermal/cooling response, energy, carbon, and cost under several scheduling policies (baseline first-fit, consolidation, thermal-aware, carbon-aware, price-aware, DVFS-aware, and an integrated-coordination policy), compared across synthetic and trace-shaped scenarios. See helicyn-sim/docs/model_assumptions.md and helicyn-sim/docs/limitations.md for the full assumption set.

Limitations

What this research does not yet show.

  • All results to date are simulated under documented modeling assumptions -- not measured from live production infrastructure.
  • The simulator is an independent research prototype (helicyn-sim), not connected to any customer's real facility, telemetry, or billing systems.
  • No production savings, verified real-world impact, or live deployments currently exist.
  • The optional external Helicyn ML integration uses a teacher-imitation-only model, not one trained on real or simulated outcomes -- see helicyn-sim/docs/ml_integration_plan.md.
  • The final paper will state its own limitations explicitly; this list will be reviewed and superseded by that document.
